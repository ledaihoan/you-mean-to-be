## The Fire Inside

Every second, inside trillions of your cells, a fire is burning. Not a dramatic blaze — a slow, controlled chemical reaction that releases just enough energy to keep you alive. Your body is the most sophisticated calorie-burning machine ever evolved: efficient enough to run for a century on a few hundred pounds of food per month, powerful enough to climb mountains or sprint from danger.

The word "metabolism" comes from the Greek *metabole* — change. Your metabolism is the sum of every chemical reaction that transforms food into life. When you eat a piece of bread, you are not just satisfying hunger. You are feeding a furnace that has been burning inside you since before you were born.

## ATP: The Cellular Currency

The key molecule in metabolism is ATP — adenosine triphosphate. Think of ATP as a tiny rechargeable battery that your cells use to power everything. Every action in your body — a heartbeat, a thought, a step — costs a few ATP molecules. And every ATP molecule costs a few calories from the food you eat.

ATP works through a simple cycle: it carries energy, delivers it where needed, then gets recharged. Your body recycles its entire body weight in ATP every day. You are not storing energy — you are cycling through it at enormous volume, like a city cycling through its water supply thousands of times per day.

## The Three-Stage Furnace

When you eat, food doesn't go straight to energy. It passes through three stages of processing:

**Digestion (the breakdown):** Enzymes in your stomach and intestines break proteins into amino acids, fats into fatty acids, and carbohydrates into glucose. Each gram of each gets broken down separately, following different paths.

**Glycolysis (the spark):** In the cytoplasm of every cell, glucose is split into pyruvate, producing a small amount of ATP. This is ancient — even single-celled bacteria do it. It doesn't require oxygen. This is the "inefficient" path that some cancer cells exploit (they favor glycolysis even when oxygen is available, a phenomenon called the Warburg effect).

**The Krebs cycle and electron transport chain (the blaze):** This is where the real energy extraction happens. Pyruvate enters the mitochondria — the organelles that were once free-living bacteria that merged with our ancestors a billion years ago. Through a cascade of reactions (Krebs cycle) and a chain of protein complexes (electron transport chain), your cells extract up to 36 ATP molecules from a single glucose molecule. That's 36 times more than glycolysis alone.

The electron transport chain is essentially a series of "checkpoints" where electrons are passed along, releasing energy at each step. The energy is used to pump hydrogen ions across a membrane, creating a gradient. When those ions flow back through special channels, they drive ATP synthesis. This is why we breathe oxygen — it is the final electron acceptor, the one that keeps the chain running.

## Why Fat Has More Calories Per Gram

Protein and carbohydrates each yield 4 calories per gram. Fat yields 9 calories per gram. This isn't arbitrary — it reflects the chemical energy density of each molecule.

Fat is more energy-dense because it is a more reduced form of carbon. Carbohydrates are already partially oxidized (plants photosynthesize by adding oxygen). Fat is more "unburned" — it has more potential energy stored in its carbon-hydrogen bonds. When you burn fat, you are releasing more energy per carbon atom than when you burn carbohydrates.

This is why our bodies store excess energy as fat rather than as glycogen (the storage form of carbohydrates): fat stores about 6 times more energy per gram, and fat tissue also requires less water to maintain than glycogen (glycogen binds water, adding bulk).

## The Mitochondrial Legacy

Here's something remarkable: your mitochondria have their own DNA, distinct from the DNA in your cell nucleus. This is because mitochondria were once free-living bacteria — probably a type of purple non-sulfur bacteria — that merged with an ancestral cell about 2 billion years ago. The merger was so beneficial that both organisms became inseparable.

This explains why mitochondria still do many things their own way. They have 37 genes (versus 20,000+ in the nucleus). They divide on their own schedule. They are inherited only from your mother (sperm mitochondria are destroyed after fertilization). And they are the reason we age: mitochondrial DNA accumulates mutations over time, and when enough mitochondria fail, the cell follows.

Your metabolism is, in a very real sense, a partnership between two ancient organisms that learned to work together.

## Why Carnivores, Herbivores, and Omnivores Exist

Different digestive systems are not accidents — they reflect different evolutionary solutions to the same energy problem.

**Herbivores** face abundant but low-quality food. Grass and leaves are everywhere, but they are full of cellulose (a glucose polymer that most animals cannot digest) and often contain toxins. Herbivores solve this with long digestive tracts, fermentation chambers (cows have four stomachs), and gut bacteria that can break down cellulose. They trade speed of digestion for efficiency of extraction.

**Carnivores** face scarce but high-quality food. One meal can sustain them for days. Their digestive tracts are short — they don't need to extract maximum energy because each meal delivers so much. They invest energy in hunting skills rather than digestive machinery.

**Omnivores** (us) occupy the middle ground. Our digestive system is versatile: we can extract energy from both plants and animals. This flexibility is why humans adapted to almost every environment on Earth. The Inuit thrive on a high-fat animal diet; agricultural societies thrive on plants. Both work because our metabolism is general-purpose.
